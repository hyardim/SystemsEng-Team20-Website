Project background and goals

The challenge

The NHS Advice and Guidance process allows GPs to seek non-urgent specialist input before deciding whether a patient needs referral, further investigation or a change in management. In principle, it is intended to help patients receive the right next step without unnecessary delay, while making better use of specialist time.

In the current Advice and Guidance process, GPs who need specialist advice often have to prepare requests manually, explain the case in free text and wait for a specialist to review and respond. Even when the question is routine, turnaround can still take a long time, which slows clinical decision-making and can contribute to longer patient wait times before the right action is taken. For patients, this can mean delays in reassurance, treatment decisions, referrals or follow-up planning while clinicians wait for the information they need.

The process is also costly for specialists. Consultants can spend hours of their time manually reading incoming queries, searching through NICE and BSR guidance, finding the relevant sections to support their answer and then drafting replies with appropriate citations. Many of these queries are repetitive, but they still require careful manual handling, which takes time away from more complex cases while keeping pressure high on both speed and safety.

This means the existing workflow is slow for GPs, labour-intensive for specialists and difficult to scale without compromising traceability, evidence quality or clinical oversight. The impact is ultimately felt by patients, because slower advice and slower access to specialist input can delay the point at which the right next step is taken in their care.

Our solution

Our solution is to create a system that makes the Advice and Guidance process more structured, transparent and efficient for both GPs and specialists. At a high level, the platform should help GPs submit clearer requests, help specialists deal with routine cases more efficiently and make relevant guidance easier to surface and reference. A key part of this is the use of retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). In practice, this means the model first retrieves the most relevant guideline content and then uses that material to produce its answer. This helps keep answers grounded in the most relevant guideline material rather than being generated without clear supporting evidence.

The drafted requirements therefore focus on reducing repetitive manual work, improving evidence visibility and preserving human oversight. The system should support structured submissions, evidence-linked responses, specialist review before advice is returned and clear safe fallback behaviour when a case is uncertain, high risk or outside scope.

From a patient perspective, this should mean guidance can be produced and checked more quickly, reducing the time patients wait for the next decision in their care. In practice, that means faster reassurance where appropriate, earlier movement towards the right investigation, treatment or referral and less uncertainty while clinicians wait for specialist input.

Technical goals Solving existing problems
  • Build a secure multi-role consultation platform for GP, Specialist, and Admin users with authentication, role-based access control, and audit visibility.
  • Implement a grounded RAG pipeline that retrieves the right NICE and BSR guidance sections, generates cited answers, and keeps evidence visible in the workflow.
  • Support specialist oversight through assignment, review, rejection or replacement of AI answers, and safe fallback behaviour when evidence is weak or the case is high risk.
  • Deliver the system as a reproducible multi-service stack using a React frontend, FastAPI services, PostgreSQL with pgvector, Redis, and Docker Compose.
Non-technical goals The bigger picture
  • Improve GP access to accurate, guideline-based answers for routine queries.
  • Reduce repetitive specialist workload through safe automation and more efficient review of routine cases.
  • Reduce patient wait time by helping routine cases be handled faster and complex cases receive specialist attention sooner.
  • Maintain trust through visible citations and clear evidence linkage.
  • Ensure outputs remain clinically appropriate and easy to review.

Operational success measure: in representative routine-case walkthroughs, the system should reduce time to a usable specialist-reviewed response compared with manual guideline lookup alone.

Requirement gathering

How did we collect requirements?

We gathered requirements through direct interviews and meetings with two client specialists in Rheumatology and Neurology, together with regular supervisor and partner checkpoints. We then cross-checked the resulting workflow assumptions and evidence needs against NICE and BSR guidance.

What evidence was used?

Requirement decisions were grounded in responses from the conducted interviews, workflow walkthroughs, prototype review feedback, the short validation survey, internal scoping work, and cross-checking against the relevant NICE and BSR guideline set.

Did we design a survey?

Yes. We designed a short survey with 7 items: five Likert-scale questions (trust, clarity, speed, citation usefulness, and escalation clarity) and two open-text questions for concerns and missing features.

How did we analyse and prioritise?

We coded recurring themes from interview and survey feedback (speed, trust, safe fallback, transparency), then mapped these into MoSCoW priorities with explicit acceptance criteria.

Interview summary with two specialists (open each question)

The interviews focused on real Advice & Guidance pain points and specialist workload. Each question maps directly to requirement decisions.

Q1. When do you need support most during consultation?

Support is most useful for routine but time-sensitive questions where the right guidance is buried in long documents. This informed the need for fast, concise answers with visible citations.

Q2. What makes an AI answer trustworthy in clinical contexts?

Trust depended on seeing where answers came from, how current guidance was, and knowing there would be a human check before anything was submitted back to the GP. This informed citation placement, source metadata, and specialist review controls.

Q3. What should happen when evidence is weak, conflicting, or out of scope?

Specialists preferred refusal and specialist review over speculation. This directly supported low-confidence fallback behavior where uncertain cases stay in the specialist-reviewed workflow before any response is sent back.

Q4. What details should a GP provide for a useful first response?

A useful first response depends on clear question framing, short context, relevant specialty, and optional supporting files. This supported structured consultation input with attachment capability.

Q5. What paper trail is needed for governance and reliability?

NHS-facing workflows required a visible record of who asked, reviewed, edited, and approved each response, with timestamps and status changes. This informed consultation history and audit requirements.

Q6. How should answers be presented to reduce cognitive load?

Specialists wanted key guidance and citations to be easy to scan, with review status and next-step actions clearly visible. This informed usability and interface clarity requirements.

Final workflow after requirement refinement

The flowchart below reflects the final workflow after requirements were revised through specialist feedback, workflow review, and technical constraints. Guidance is generated in the consultation chat and reviewed in that same flow before anything is returned to the GP. After the GP submits a new consultation, the chat opens with the submitted question already shown as the first message.

The final workflow above is the one used for the remainder of this Requirements page. In the revised workflow, a GP submits a structured consultation, the system produces an evidence-linked draft in the same chat, and a specialist reviews that draft from the unassigned queue by opening the query, assigning it, then choosing whether to approve, approve with comment, request changes, provide a manual response, or edit the message before any checked response is returned to the GP. Optional file uploads support submission, while notifications and admin monitoring support the wider platform workflow.

Alignment with the delivered system

The delivered repository confirms that the implemented platform extends beyond core answer generation. Alongside grounded retrieval, citations, refusal handling, and specialist review before GP-facing output, the system includes authenticated account flows, consultation file attachments, notifications, hybrid local/cloud provider routing, and protected admin monitoring pages.

These capabilities remain part of the requirements baseline because they support governance, usability, and safe operation of the clinical workflow rather than speculative extras.

Required admin functionality

Admin workflows are required for governance and oversight. Authorised admins should inspect users, consultation and chat histories, audit logs, status changes, and timestamps so there is a clear operational paper trail across the platform, alongside summary monitoring of failures and routing behavior.

Scope boundaries and exclusions

  • The platform is specialised for neurology and rheumatology guideline assistance rather than general-purpose medical chat.
  • The workflow is predominantly text-based; supporting files can be attached, but broad multimodal reasoning remains out of scope.
  • Polling is used for updates instead of websockets, which simplifies deployment but trades off push responsiveness.
  • Cloud-assisted inference is available only as controlled fallback while local-first processing remains the default.

Constraint summary

A coursework project must show end-to-end engineering depth, so the system balances a real deployment shape with pragmatic shortcuts such as startup schema patching and an evolving RAG module layout.

Intel pivot note: Early planning assumed continuous access to an Intel-backed inference server. After access ended, requirements were updated to keep local-first processing while permitting controlled cloud fallback and provider switching based on query characteristics.
