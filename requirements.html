Project requirements

This page outlines the partner context, project goals, requirement-gathering methods, personas, use cases and the prioritised functional and non-functional requirements for Ambience-AI-1.5.

NHS Clinical Context

Project background

The challenge

The NHS Advice and Guidance process allows GPs to seek non-urgent specialist input before deciding whether a patient needs referral, further investigation or a change in management. In principle, it is intended to help patients receive the right next step without unnecessary delay, while making better use of specialist time.

In the current Advice and Guidance process, GPs who need specialist advice often have to prepare requests manually, explain the case in free text and wait for a specialist to review and respond. Even when the question is routine, turnaround can still take a long time, which slows clinical decision-making and can contribute to longer patient wait times before the right action is taken. For patients, this can mean delays in reassurance, treatment decisions, referrals or follow-up planning while clinicians wait for the information they need.

The process is also costly for specialists. Consultants can spend hours of their time manually reading incoming queries, searching through NICE and BSR guidance, finding the relevant sections to support their answer and then drafting replies with appropriate citations. Many of these queries are repetitive, but they still require careful manual handling, which takes time away from more complex cases while keeping pressure high on both speed and safety.

This means the existing workflow is slow for GPs, labour-intensive for specialists and difficult to scale without compromising traceability, evidence quality or clinical oversight. The impact is ultimately felt by patients, because slower advice and slower access to specialist input can delay the point at which the right next step is taken in their care.

Our solution

Our solution is to create a system that makes the Advice and Guidance process more structured, transparent and efficient for both GPs and specialists. At a high level, the platform should help GPs submit clearer requests, help specialists deal with routine cases more efficiently and make relevant guidance easier to surface and reference. A key part of this is the use of retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). In practice, this means the model first retrieves the most relevant guideline content and then uses that material to produce its answer. This helps keep answers grounded in the most relevant guideline material rather than being generated without clear supporting evidence.

The drafted requirements therefore focus on reducing repetitive manual work, improving evidence visibility and preserving human oversight. The system should support structured submissions, evidence-linked responses, specialist review before advice is returned and clear safe fallback behaviour when a case is uncertain, high risk or outside scope.

From a patient perspective, this should mean guidance can be produced and checked more quickly, reducing the time patients wait for the next decision in their care. In practice, that means faster reassurance where appropriate, earlier movement towards the right investigation, treatment or referral and less uncertainty while clinicians wait for specialist input.

Project goals

Technical goals
  • Deploy a domain-adapted Med42 runtime with efficient local-first inference and controlled cloud fallback.
  • Build a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) pipeline that surfaces the correct NICE and BSR guideline sections.
  • Implement safety mechanisms for refusal when evidence is insufficient.
  • Provide a clean, accessible web interface aligned with NHS usability guidance.
Non-technical goals
  • Improve GP access to accurate, guideline-based answers for routine queries.
  • Reduce repetitive specialist workload through safe automation and more efficient review of routine cases.
  • Reduce patient wait time by helping routine cases be handled faster and complex cases receive specialist attention sooner.
  • Maintain trust through visible citations and clear evidence linkage.
  • Ensure outputs remain clinically appropriate and easy to review.

Operational success measure: in representative routine-case walkthroughs, the system should reduce time to a usable specialist-reviewed response compared with manual guideline lookup alone.

Requirement gathering

The final requirements below reflect the baseline agreed after iterative refinement during the project. We gathered requirements through interview-led collaboration with two client-side specialists in Rheumatology and Neurology and regular check-ins with project partners, then refined them as the workflow, prototypes and technical constraints became clearer.

How did we collect requirements?

We met directly with two client specialists in Rheumatology and Neurology, both highly experienced specialists in their fields, then refined requirements through regular partner check-ins and guideline-driven validation against NICE/BSR documents.

What evidence was used?

Requirement decisions were grounded in client meetings, internal scoping work, workflow and prototype reviews and validation against the relevant guideline set.

How did we analyse and prioritise?

We coded recurring themes from specialist interviews and prototype feedback (speed, trust, safe fallback, transparency), then converted those into MoSCoW priorities with explicit acceptance criteria.

Interview summary with two specialists (open each question)

The interviews focused on real Advice & Guidance workflow pain points and specialist workload. Each question maps directly to requirement decisions.

Q1. When do you need support most during consultation?

Support is most useful for routine but time-sensitive questions where the correct guidance is buried in long documents. This informed the need for fast, focused answers with visible citations.

Q2. What makes an AI answer trustworthy in clinical contexts?

Trust depended on seeing where the answer came from, how current the guidance was and knowing there would be a human check before anything was submitted back to the GP. This informed the requirement for clear citations, source metadata and specialist review.

Q3. What should happen when evidence is weak, conflicting or outside scope?

The specialists preferred refusal and specialist review over speculation. This directly supported a low-confidence fallback path in which uncertain cases stay within the specialist-reviewed workflow before any response is sent back.

Q4. What details should a GP provide so the system can return a useful first response?

A useful first response depends on a clear question, short clinical context, the relevant specialty and any supporting files. This supported structured consultation input and optional attachments.

Q5. What paper trail is needed for governance, security and reliability?

The NHS-facing workflow needed a clear record of who asked, reviewed, edited and approved each response, together with timestamps and visible status changes. This informed consultation history, review traceability and admin audit requirements.

Q6. How should the answer be presented so it is easy to act on quickly?

The specialists wanted key guidance and citations to be easy to scan, with review status and next-step information clearly visible. This informed the usability and workflow presentation requirements.

Final workflow after requirement refinement

The flowchart below reflects the final workflow after the requirements were revised through specialist feedback, workflow review and technical constraints. Guidance is generated within the consultation chat and then reviewed in that same consultation flow before anything is returned to the GP. After the GP completes the new consultation form and submits it, the consultation chat opens automatically with the submitted question already shown as the first message.

The final workflow above is the one used for the remainder of this Requirements page. In that revised workflow, a GP submits a structured consultation, the system produces an evidence-linked draft in the same chat and a specialist reviews that draft from the unassigned queue by opening the query, assigning it to themselves and then choosing whether to approve, approve with comment, request changes, provide a manual response or edit the message before any checked response is returned to the GP. Optional file uploads support submission, while notifications and admin monitoring support the wider platform workflow.

Alignment with the delivered system

The final delivered repository confirms that the implemented platform extends beyond the clinical answer flow alone. In addition to grounded retrieval, citations, refusal handling and specialist review before responses are returned to the GP, the delivered system includes authenticated account flows, consultation file attachments, notification handling, hybrid local/cloud provider routing and protected admin monitoring pages.

These capabilities are reflected in the requirements because they are part of the working product rather than speculative extras: they support governance, usability and safe deployment of the core clinical workflow.

Required admin functionality

Although the admin panel is not part of the main clinician flow, it is still required for governance and operational oversight. Authorised admins should be able to inspect users, consultation and chat histories, audit logs, status changes and timestamps so there is a clear paper trail of who said what and when, alongside summary monitoring of failures and routing behaviour.

Scope boundaries and exclusions

  • The platform is specialised for neurology and rheumatology guideline assistance rather than general-purpose medical chat.
  • The current workflow is predominantly text-based; supporting files can be attached to consultations, but broad multimodal reasoning remains outside the intended scope.
  • Polling is used for updates instead of websockets, which simplifies deployment but trades off push responsiveness.
  • Cloud-assisted inference is available as controlled fallback while local-first execution remains the default path.

Constraint summary

A coursework project must show end-to-end engineering depth, so the system balances a real deployment shape with pragmatic shortcuts such as startup schema patching and an evolving RAG module layout.

Intel pivot note: Early project planning assumed continuous access to an Intel-backed inference server. After access ended, requirements were updated to keep local-first processing while permitting controlled cloud fallback and automatic provider switching based on query characteristics.

Ambience-AI-1.5

A technical systems report for a university software engineering project. The system under study is a role-aware clinical consultation platform that combines React, FastAPI, PostgreSQL with pgvector and a grounded medical RAG service.

This website intentionally describes implementation details, deployment paths, safeguards, testing and known limitations rather than product marketing claims.
