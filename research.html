Research

Related systems, evidence, and technical choices

Related Projects Review

We reviewed existing medical AI systems to understand what strong clinical assistants already do well. The most useful patterns were consistent: evidence grounding, structured reasoning, and clear signalling that the system supports clinicians rather than replacing them.

OpenEvidence

OpenEvidence is a clinician-facing medical search and synthesis product built around evidence visibility rather than free-form chatbot fluency [1][2]. For Ambience, the most useful lesson was that trust comes from keeping sources in the main answer flow instead of treating references as an optional extra.

Main features

  • Clinician-focused search over medical evidence and guidance.
  • Source attribution remains attached to the answer itself.
  • Designed as a professional workflow tool rather than a generic consumer assistant.

What Ambience learned

  • Citations should be visible at the same level as the answer text.
  • The retrieved evidence should feel like part of the product, not a hidden backend step.
  • A narrow clinician-facing scope makes the system easier to justify and evaluate.

References

  • [1] OpenEvidence, "About."
  • [2] OpenEvidence, "New Feature: Why Was This Source Cited?"

Glass Health

Glass Health is a stronger comparison on output structure. Its clinical Q&A, differential diagnosis, and plan-generation workflows show how medical AI becomes more usable when responses are explicitly organised for scanning and review [3].

Main features

  • Structured differential diagnosis and assessment-and-plan generation.
  • Evidence-backed answers with inline numbered citations.
  • Clinical workflow emphasis rather than a single plain chat box.

What Ambience learned

  • Structured outputs reduce scanning effort for busy clinicians.
  • Reasoning should be grouped into meaningful sections rather than one long paragraph.
  • The UI should make escalation and review feel normal, not exceptional.

References

  • [3] Glass Health, "Features."

Med-PaLM 2

Med-PaLM 2 is not a directly comparable deployed product, but it is an important research reference because it frames medical LLM quality in terms of safety, helpfulness, and clinician preference rather than raw model fluency alone [4][5].

Main features

  • Medical question-answering research with formal human evaluation.
  • Measures quality across long-form answers, safety, and medical consensus.
  • Treats deployment readiness as a separate question from benchmark strength.

What Ambience learned

  • High-quality answers still need explicit safety boundaries before real use.
  • Capability research should not be confused with autonomous clinical readiness.
  • Human specialist oversight remains the clearest safety layer in our final design.

References

  • [4] Google Research, "Med-PaLM."
  • [5] K. Singhal et al., "Toward expert-level medical question answering with large language models."

Cross-project takeaway

Across all three examples, the strongest pattern was the same: good clinical AI does not just answer questions, it grounds them in evidence, structures them for quick review, and leaves the final judgement with human clinicians.

Technology Review

The final implementation in the sibling Ambience repository uses three services (frontend, backend, and rag_service) plus PostgreSQL, Redis, and optional nginx. The research question was not simply which tools were popular, but which combination created the most grounded, testable, and clinically defensible workflow.

Solution architecture options

Option Strengths Weaknesses Decision
Plain LLM chatbot Lowest implementation effort and fastest initial prototype. No explicit provenance, weak update path for clinical knowledge, and higher hallucination risk. Rejected for a safety-critical medical support workflow.
Single monolithic backend with embedded retrieval Fewer services and simpler local deployment. Tightly couples authentication, chat CRUD, ingestion, retrieval, and model runtime concerns. Viable, but harder to evolve and test cleanly as the RAG pipeline grows.
Dedicated RAG service with grounded generation [6] Separates ingestion, retrieval, routing, and generation from the general application backend. Introduces more moving parts and service boundaries. Chosen. It matches the implemented repository structure and keeps clinical retrieval logic isolated from account and workflow APIs.

On the "device" decision

Ambience is a browser-based system, so there is no special hardware client in the usual sense. The meaningful deployment comparison was where model inference should run: locally, in the cloud, or through a hybrid route.

Model-hosting options

Option Benefits Risks Decision
Local-only Ollama + Med42 [7][8] Low marginal cost, easier offline development path, and better control over data locality. Performance depends on local hardware and smaller models can struggle on harder cases. Useful for development and low-cost baseline inference.
Cloud-only hosted medical LLM [7] Better capacity for complex prompts and no need for local GPU resources. Higher cost, external dependency, and stronger privacy/governance requirements. Useful, but too rigid as the only path.
Hybrid routed local/cloud inference Balances cost and capability while enabling fallback between providers. Adds routing heuristics and more operational complexity. Chosen. The implemented router scores complexity, prompt size, clinical risk, and retrieval ambiguity before deciding whether to escalate to cloud inference.

Retrieval and ranking algorithms

Approach Why it helps Main limitation Decision
Keyword-only search Strong lexical precision for known terms, abbreviations, and exact phrases. Misses semantically similar wording and paraphrases. Kept as one signal, but not sufficient alone.
Vector-only semantic search [9][12] Captures paraphrase and conceptual similarity better than exact matching. Can over-retrieve semantically related but clinically unhelpful passages. Also valuable, but not robust enough on its own.
Hybrid retrieval + RRF + reranking [10][11] Combines lexical and semantic recall, then re-orders candidates with a stronger relevance model. More stages to tune and slightly higher latency. Chosen. The implemented pipeline uses sentence-aware chunking, vector and keyword search, reciprocal rank fusion, filtering, cross-encoder reranking, deduplication, and citation assembly.

Languages, frameworks, libraries, and APIs

Layer Options reviewed Choice Why it was chosen
Frontend React SPA with Vite vs heavier SSR-first alternatives React 19 + TypeScript + Vite [13][14] Ambience is dashboard-oriented rather than SEO-driven, so a typed SPA gives enough structure without taking on unnecessary server-rendering complexity.
Backend API FastAPI vs larger full-stack Python web frameworks FastAPI [15] Fits async API workloads well, provides validation through Pydantic, and exposes interactive OpenAPI documentation for quick testing.
Data + retrieval store PostgreSQL with pgvector vs separate specialist vector database PostgreSQL + pgvector [12] Keeps operational data and retrieval data in one database family, while still supporting vector similarity search and standard relational tooling.
Inference interface Provider-specific clients vs an OpenAI-compatible abstraction Ollama/OpenAI-compatible endpoints [8] Lets the same service swap between local and cloud providers with less integration churn.

Summary of Technical Decisions

The final research outcome was a deliberately constrained clinical support system rather than an unrestricted medical assistant. The technical decisions below are the ones that most strongly shape the final implementation.

Grounding first

Answers are produced from retrieved guideline material and returned with explicit citations, not as unsupported model free text.

Separate RAG service

Ingestion, retrieval, reranking, routing, and generation live outside the main backend so they can be tested and evolved independently.

Hybrid inference path

Local Med42 is viable for routine development and lower-cost requests, while harder or riskier prompts can be escalated to a cloud path.

Hybrid retrieval pipeline

Keyword search and vector search are fused, reranked, deduplicated, and converted into citation objects before the answer is shown to the user.

  • The implemented frontend uses React, TypeScript, Vite, Tailwind CSS, and route-based role-aware views.
  • The implemented backend uses FastAPI, SQLAlchemy, and Redis-backed caching for read-heavy workflows.
  • The RAG service uses sentence-transformers, pgvector, cross-encoder reranking, Redis + RQ retry jobs, and OpenAI-compatible local or cloud model providers.
  • Specialist review remains part of the normal workflow, which is the clearest safety boundary in the overall system design.

Ambience-AI-1.5

A technical systems report for a university software engineering project. The system under study is a role-aware clinical consultation platform that combines React, FastAPI, PostgreSQL with pgvector, and a grounded medical RAG service.

This website intentionally describes implementation details, deployment paths, safeguards, testing, and known limitations rather than product marketing claims.
